The Migration of Art

This desktop prototype explores how we can visualize an artwork's travels across the globe through ownership changes and exhibition displays, focusing on The Met's collection of Van Gogh paintings.

How does art travel across the globe before it arrives in a museum gallery? When was it bought and sold, and by whom? When and where was it sent on exhibition tours? Studying an artwork's rich history of movement is crucial to understanding how the world interacted with a piece, along with how it influenced the broader world of art and human culture. While a student of data visualization at Parsons School of Design, I worked with wonderful partners at The Metropolitan Museum of Art to explore these questions in a visual context, utilizing both an abstracted visual timeline and animated mapping to follow the museum's collection of Van Gogh paintings through their paths traveled from creation to their current location at The Met.

Process

Data Source: The Met API, made available to our course.
Tools: Python and pandas for collecting, cleaning, and aggregating data. d3, jQuery, JavaScript, HTML/CSS, and node.js for web development and prototyping.

Data

I was able to access the provenance and exhibition history of each piece in this project through an API provided by our partners at The Met. My main data challenge in this project was how to parse this information, which was organized as separate paragraphs of text and lists, into workable structures that brought these data together into one cohesive story of movement for each painting:

After bringing down each provenance paragraph and exhibition history from The Met's API into static files, I needed to split the paragraphs of provenance text into individual unique ownership tenures, associating each owner the with the location listed and timeframe of ownership. I then joined exhibitions and provenance together into the same timeline, logging the location of a painting in each year from creation through 2017 (the most recent data available). In my model, a painting moves locations any time it was acquired by a new owner or went on exhibition. After a painting's last exhibition, it returns to The Met. I used pandas for this data processing and aggregating into data frames, which I then exported as JSON files to be loaded into my front-end visualization.

Visualization

The Migration of Art starts with a full-screen animation of each painting included in the project moving across the screen, orienting us to the project's concept of exploring how these pieces move from place to place. These paintings will become the menu with which we navigate and filter the project's main visualizations.

The project introduction, reminscient of a placard next to next to each painting on the walls of a museum, orients us to the context of the project.

Our main visualization focuses on an abstract representation of each painting's movement. Each location visited by at least one painting in our analysis gets a horizontal line on our y-axis, arranged from east (top) to west (bottom). These locations range from cities to specific Museums. Along the x-axis we track each year in the painting's lifespan, allowing us to see the painting's full history of movement in one visual.

This abstract timeline is supported by our map view, showing the path each painting traveled across the globe from its creation through 2017. Through animation we can walk through each year of this journey, following along with these travels in real time.
