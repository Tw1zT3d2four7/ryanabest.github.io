mind the gap.

This visualization explores the political strategy of gerrymandering, how we can measure it, and what role it played in the 2012 US Federal Congressional elections.

 

See the prototype →



Gerrymandering is an ingrained facet of one of the most fundamental aspects of American democracy - how we draw the districts that elect our representatives. It's a political strategy that's often blamed for creating uncompetitive elections or stoking political divisiveness and has received quite a bit of attention from national publications. This visualization dives into how gerrymandering works, what methods we can use quantifiably measure this phenomenon, and what these metrics can tell us about its effect in American elections. This analysis specifically focuses on the 2012 Federal Congressional elections, measuring the efficiency gap and difference from proportional representation to find evidence of its political influence stemming from significant redistricting that followed the 2010 census.

Data Source: FEC 2012 Senate and House Results

Tools: Javascript and node.js for cleaning/aggregating data and web prototyping. jQuery and Flexbox for state and district visualizations. p5.js, JavaScript, and HTML/CSS for additional web development. Adobe Illustrator for concept development and visual prototyping.

Data

The data I used in this project comes from a 2012 federal congressional election results excel file available on the FEC website. I first needed to do clean-up in excel to standardize some undeeded inconsistencies and reduce this data to only those candidates who won their district's primaries in the year's house elections.

I then used node.js to further process this data, leveraging the power of js libraries that would also be used in the page's visualization, including p5, jQuery, and Lodash. I needed to organize district results, tracking the votes and percentage of votes cast for each candidate compared with the amount of votes needed to win that district, and calculate state-wide results of my two main metrics for each party: the efficiency gap and the difference in seats earned and seats expected with pure proportionality. This data processing generated two clean json files, one for district results and one for state, that I fed into my front-end visualization.
